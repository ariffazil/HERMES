# People Research & Cultural Decoding — PETRONAS Context

## Purpose

When Arif names a person + institution and asks "decode this session" or "tell me about X", this is NOT a LinkedIn search. It's **institutional people forensics** — understanding who someone is, what they represent in the organizational ecosystem, and what their behavior signals about the institution's state.

## Research Protocol

### Step 1 — Profile build (minimum viable)

For any named person in a Malaysian O&G / institutional context:

1. **Web search** — name + role + organization. OpenClaw web_search if Tavily is down.
2. **LinkedIn** — career trajectory, education, specialties (extract via web search if LinkedIn blocks direct access)
3. **Conference speaker pages** — iCEP, EAGE, AAPG, SEG. These reveal: current title, scope of responsibility, technical specialties, institutional positioning.
4. **Publication history** — ResearchGate, Google Scholar, Search and Discovery. PhD thesis = window into technical worldview.
5. **Social proof** — Instagram, company media releases, photos from events. Reveals: how the institution markets this person, how visible they are.

### Step 2 — Decode the profile into institutional signals

| Signal | What it tells you |
|--------|-------------------|
| PhD under famous supervisor | Technical credibility, network access, institutional capital |
| Internal trainer/coach role | Knowledge transfer commitment, mentoring orientation |
| "Head of X" vs "Manager of X" | Strategic vs operational positioning |
| Career path: pure technical → leadership | Technical depth preserved, not just political climbers |
| Conference speaker invitations | Institutional willingness to showcase this person externally |
| Multiple title variations in search | Title churn, reorganization, or dual-hatting |

### Step 3 — Contextualize the meeting/session

When Arif describes a session ("orang kampung style", "like father and sons"), decode:

1. **Communication style** → What it signals about the person's position and intent
   - "Orang kampung style" = authenticity, no performance, established power (doesn't need to impress)
   - "Like father and sons" = paternalistic mentoring, knowledge transfer, possibly legacy session
   - Cynical about management = friction between technical depth and current leadership direction
   
2. **Audience selection** → Why these people?
   - Senior exec (not junior, not C-suite) = investing in next generation of decision-makers
   - 15 people = intimate enough for real talk, large enough to plant seeds widely
   - "Engagement" = buy-in session, not presentation

3. **Content choice** → What was NOT said
   - Personal stories (childhood, ambition) = building trust before delivering uncomfortable truths
   - "Why geology matters" = defending core identity against institutional drift
   - No formal slides = the message IS the informality — "I trust you enough to be real"

### Step 4 — Connect to institutional DNA

For PETRONAS specifically, the founding values article (The Edge Malaysia, "The PetronAS DNA", Feb 2010) is the canonical reference:
- Integrity as culture, not poster
- "We" over "my" — Azizan's shared values
- "Fight like bulldogs" — Rastam's discipline
- PSC revolution — from concession to production sharing
- Trust with government — "the day we compromise on integrity will be the day we see Petronas destroyed"

When a senior technical leader "embodies the spirit" — they represent these founding values. Their cynicism about current management = they see the DNA eroding.

## The "Father and Sons" Pattern

This pattern appears when:
- A senior technical leader is near end of career or transition
- They feel institutional responsibility to transmit values, not just knowledge
- The formal channels (training, KPI, succession planning) don't capture what matters
- They choose informal, personal storytelling as the vehicle

**What to listen for:**
- Personal vulnerability (childhood, failures, doubts) = building trust
- "When I was your age" stories = mapping their path for the audience to consider
- Cynicism about management = naming what they see as wrong, indirectly
- Defending core disciplines ("geology matters") = protecting institutional identity

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't treat this as just "soft skills" or "mentoring" — it's institutional survival behavior
- Don't reduce it to personality — it's a signal about the institution's state
- Don't dismiss the cynicism as bitterness — it's pattern recognition from someone with 20+ years of observation
