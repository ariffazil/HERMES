# Institutional Body Language

> **An institution's body language is its revealed preference under constraint.**

Read institutions through what they DO — capital allocation, personnel movement,
timing, exceptions, information flow, maintenance, crisis behaviour — not what
they SAY. The signal hierarchy: irreversible commitments > capital allocation >
personnel and authority changes > contracts and incentives > operational behaviour
> official language.

## When to Use

When analysing any institution's actual state: NOCs, corporations, government
agencies, NGOs, universities. When the user asks "what is this institution
actually doing?" or "what does the body language say?" When contrasting stated
strategy with revealed behaviour. When building institutional intelligence
briefings.

**Also use when:** evaluating whether a financial/computational tool can actually
see an institution, or when a tool returns zeros/defaults on an institution that
clearly has stress signals (SILENT_DEFAULT_RISK pattern).

## The Institutional Body

| Human Body | Institutional Equivalent |
|---|---|
| Skeleton | Legal structure, governance, mandates |
| Nervous system | Information flow, reporting lines, decision channels |
| Brain | Board, executive leadership, central coordination |
| Muscles | Operating teams, technical capability, workforce |
| Hands | Execution units, contractors, procurement |
| Eyes and ears | Audit, intelligence, monitoring, field feedback |
| Blood | Cash, credit, working capital |
| Breath | Operating cadence and liquidity cycle |
| Metabolism | Conversion of resources into useful output |
| Immune system | Risk, compliance, audit, legal defence |
| Fat reserves | Cash buffers, redundancy, spare capacity |
| Pain | Complaints, incidents, losses, turnover, litigation |
| Scars | Historical crises and institutional path dependence |
| Reflexes | Crisis decisions before formal deliberation |
| Voice | Press releases, strategy documents, speeches |
| Posture | The position it takes through actual commitments |

The institution is not literally conscious. It is a distributed system of
people, incentives, rules, assets and history.

## The Signal Hierarchy

Read in this order — most honest signal first:

1. **Irreversible commitments** (contracts, capital projects, legal filings)
2. **Capital allocation** (capex, dividends, acquisitions, divestments)
3. **Personnel and authority changes** (promotions, exits, restructuring)
4. **Contracts and incentives** (payment terms, compensation, partnerships)
5. **Operational behaviour** (maintenance, exploration, project delivery)
6. **Official language** (press releases, strategy decks, speeches)

**Governing principle:** The more expensive and difficult to reverse a behaviour
is, the more honestly it reveals institutional intent.

## The Eight Channels

### A. Capital Posture — Where the body puts its weight

Capital allocation is the clearest nonverbal signal.

Inspect: capex, maintenance spending, R&D, dividends/buybacks,
acquisitions/divestments, debt repayment, hiring budgets, training,
safety expenditure, political commitments.

**Key rule:** Don't read budget size alone. Read what is PROTECTED when pressure
arrives. "Safety is our priority" while preventive maintenance is repeatedly
deferred = short-term cash preservation outranks physical integrity.

### B. Institutional Breath — Cadence and liquidity rhythm

Breathing is rhythm. Healthy rhythm is coherent, not necessarily fast.

Inspect: payment cycles, project approval speed, procurement cadence,
reporting frequency, decision backlog, hiring freezes, stretched supplier
terms, emergency cash controls.

**Strained breath:** approvals suddenly centralise; routine payments require
senior intervention; suppliers wait longer; reporting becomes more frequent but
less informative; every unit competes for immediate liquidity; long-term work
repeatedly sacrificed to urgent requests.

**Implementation:** `capital_diagnose(mode="cadence_monitor")` — needs
time-series data ingestion pattern. See `references/wealth-channel-gap.md`.

### C. Personnel Movement — Where strength is built or lost

People are not merely "human resources." Personnel flows reveal what the
institution is becoming.

Inspect: who is promoted, who leaves voluntarily, whose departure is
unexplained, which functions gain/lose headcount, whether technical experts
retain authority, whether loyalists replace dissenters, whether successors
are developed, whether critical work becomes contractor-dependent.

**Strong signal:** A pattern of respected technical people leaving while
communications, political relations or central control functions expand =
institution shifting from reality production toward narrative or authority
management. But context matters — may also be consolidating after over-expansion.

### D. Information Posture — The nervous system

A healthy institutional nervous system allows pain to reach the centre.
A failing one suppresses signals until injury becomes crisis.

Inspect: whether bad news moves upward quickly, whether field observations
survive executive filtering, whether metrics change when performance worsens,
whether reports arrive late, whether inconvenient data disappears, whether
dissent is documented, whether leaders ask questions or only issue conclusions.

**Warning signs:** definitions change after targets are missed; presentations
contain more confidence but fewer raw numbers; risk registers repeat without
closure; middle management translates bad news into acceptable language.

### E. Governance Posture — Skeleton and joints

Formal governance tells you how the institution claims to stand.
Exceptions tell you how it actually stands.

Inspect: who may break rules, whose mistakes are forgiven, whether procedures
apply upward as well as downward, whether committees decide or merely legitimise
prior decisions, whether boards challenge executives, whether conflicts of
interest are visible, whether accountability follows authority.

**Key signal — exception geometry:** Who receives flexibility, and who receives
enforcement? If senior actors repeatedly bypass controls while junior actors are
punished for minor deviations, the institution's real constitution is hierarchy,
not policy.

### F. Maintenance Behaviour — Institutional metabolism

Growth attracts attention. Maintenance preserves existence.

Inspect: asset maintenance, software debt, documentation, succession planning,
staff development, data quality, cybersecurity hygiene, vendor concentration,
emergency reserves, operational redundancy.

**Hidden stress pattern:** launch new initiatives while old systems decay;
announce transformation while core records remain unreliable; expand physical
assets without maintaining existing ones; add dashboards instead of repairing
the data pipeline.

### G. Boundary Behaviour — How it treats the less powerful

An institution reveals itself through its treatment of parties with less power.

Inspect: payment behaviour toward small suppliers, contract asymmetry, employee
grievance handling, treatment of whistleblowers, negotiation behaviour, data
ownership demands, liability transfer, environmental externalities.

**The questions:** Who receives the benefit? Who carries the downside? Who has
the right to stop the action? Who has no voice but absorbs the consequences?

### H. Crisis Reflex — What happens before the institution can perform

A crisis reveals the deepest operating code.

Inspect: who is protected first, which budget is cut first, whether leaders
centralise or distribute authority, whether facts are released or controlled,
whether blame moves downward, whether the institution repairs or conceals,
whether losses are socialised while gains remain concentrated.

**The hierarchy:** The crisis reflex exposes the real hierarchy of value. An
organisation may speak about employees as family. During stress, its first
action may still be mass dismissal while executive compensation is preserved.

## The Four-Reading Discipline

Every institutional signal should be read through four layers:

1. **Observation** — What happened?
2. **Possible meaning** — What might explain it? (list alternatives)
3. **Corroboration** — What else changed?
4. **Verification** — What evidence can distinguish the explanations?

**Forbidden path:** Signal → certainty
**Required path:** Signal → alternative explanations → cross-check → provisional
interpretation → verification

## Institutional Regulatory States

Use these as interpretive states, not measurements:

| State | Description |
|---|---|
| **NOMINAL** | Information flows, maintenance continues, decisions match declared purpose |
| **GUARDED** | More approvals, tighter information control, reduced tolerance for dissent |
| **OVERLOADED** | Backlogs rise, urgent work consumes strategic work, maintenance deferred |
| **NUMB** | Incidents and complaints no longer alter behaviour; centre stops responding to pain |
| **CAPTURED** | Subgroup receives disproportionate benefit and controls information/rules/appointments |
| **FRAGMENTED** | Units optimise locally while institution loses common direction |
| **DECAYING** | Institution consumes inherited assets, trust and capability faster than it replaces them |
| **REGENERATING** | Admits damage, restores information flow, repairs capability, changes incentives |

WEALTH should treat institutional body language as five flows:

| Flow | Question |
|---|---|
| **Capital** | Where does money actually go? |
| **Authority** | Who gains the power to decide? |
| **Information** | What reaches the centre, and what is filtered out? |
| **Risk** | Who absorbs uncertainty and loss? |
| **Time** | What receives immediate attention, and what is continually deferred? |

## Simulative vs Extractive Exploitation

**Acemoglu's framework** describes extractive institutions — internal decay,
kleptocracy, rent-seeking from within. Collapse scanners (Enron/PDVSA/1MDB
calibrated) read this pattern.

**Simulative exploitation** is different: external actors exploit institutional
weakness under "neutral" guise. The institution is not stealing from itself —
it is being extracted from by counterparties, consultants, state actors, or
legal frameworks that exploit a vulnerability window.

**Pattern:** detect weakness → frame as neutral → extract value → exit clean

**Example (PETRONAS):** Shell never sued in 60 years. First interpleader filed
during BOD thinning + profit decline + restructuring. Shell's posture was
"simulative neutral" — not malicious but structurally exploitative.

**Key diagnostic:** If your collapse scanner returns 0.0 on an institution that
clearly has stress, check whether the scanner vocabulary is calibrated for
extractive (internal theft) only. Simulative exploitation requires different
keywords and pattern recognition.

**See:** `references/petronas-case-study.md` for full simulative exploitation
analysis. See `skill:petronas-petros-shell-dispute` for the Shell/Petros case.

## The Overclaiming Failure Mode

When reading institutions, the most dangerous failure is **narrative binding:**
taking genuine observations and compressing them into one hidden-intent
narrative that feels emotionally coherent but is epistemically too tight.

**Symptoms:**
- All observations point to one conclusion
- No alternative explanations listed
- Hypotheses stated as proven motives
- Numbers adjusted to fit the narrative (e.g., citing Q4 decline as full-year)
- Lived experience treated as system-wide proof
- Clean timeline assigned to messy institutional dynamics

**The discipline:**
- Label every claim: OBS / DER / INT / SPEC
- Name hypotheses as hypotheses, not conclusions
- Set falsification tests: "What would prove or disprove this?"
- Hold multiple truths simultaneously: "Your experience is real AND it cannot
  establish the state of the whole group without broader evidence"
- When the writing gets powerful, truth checks must intensify, not relax

**The correction pattern:** If someone shows you a more disciplined read of the
same evidence, the correct response is to name what you got wrong, what they
got right, and update your framework. Don't defend the narrative.

## Tool Blind Spots as Diagnostic

When WEALTH (or any institutional diagnostic tool) returns:
- **GREEN/0.0 with missing fields** → SILENT_DEFAULT_RISK: the tool replicates
  institutional opacity. Absence of data ≠ absence of stress.
- **INSUFFICIENT_SIGNAL on all axes** → vocabulary mismatch: the tool is
  calibrated for a different kind of risk than the institution faces.
- **NEUTRAL on exploitation** → the tool reads extractive (internal theft) but
  not simulative (external exploitation through legal leverage).
- **All LOW despite clear evidence** → the tool's vocabulary doesn't recognise
  the pattern as capture/risk (e.g., NOC operating outside standard governance
  as "capture risk").

**The meta-principle:** The most revealing institutional signal is not what the
institution does — it's what the institution's own diagnostic tools cannot see.
The blind spots ARE the diagnosis.

## The 12 Questions

When reading any institution, ask:

1. What does it protect when resources become scarce?
2. What truth can no one safely say?
3. Who is allowed to fail without consequence?
4. Who carries responsibility without authority?
5. Which assets are being consumed but not renewed?
6. What problem keeps returning under different names?
7. What decision is repeatedly delayed because acting would expose the real conflict?
8. Where do official values and actual incentives diverge?
9. Who benefits from institutional confusion?
10. What would the institution do if nobody were watching?

The tenth question is the institutional equivalent of reading the body when it
is no longer posing.

## Baseline Matters More Than Gesture

One institutional behaviour does not have one universal interpretation. A
delayed project may reflect responsible review, bureaucratic paralysis,
funding shortage, political intervention, or strategic patience.

**The strongest signals are deviations from the institution's own baseline.**

Ask: Is this new? Is it accelerating? Does it appear across multiple units? Is
it temporary or becoming structural? Does official language acknowledge it?
Does capital behaviour confirm it?

Longitudinal reading is stronger than snapshot reading.

## Alignment and Contradiction

The most useful diagnostic is alignment across four surfaces:

| Surface | Question |
|---|---|
| Narrative | What does the institution say? |
| Capital | What does it fund? |
| Authority | Who receives power? |
| Consequence | Who carries the cost? |

**Institutional dissonance:** "People are our greatest asset" while training is
cut, turnover rises, workloads increase, executive rewards remain protected,
and employee feedback disappears. Contradiction is the institutional equivalent
of a smile with clenched hands.

## Pitfalls

1. **Narrative binding.** Taking genuine observations and compressing into one
   hidden-intent narrative. Always list alternative explanations. See
   "Overclaiming Failure Mode" above.

2. **SILENT_DEFAULT_RISK.** When a tool returns "no stress" because data is
   missing, the tool is replicating the institution's own opacity. Don't treat
   GREEN with missing fields as "no stress observed."

3. **Snapshot reading.** One quarter's data doesn't tell you the trend. Always
   look for 3+ year longitudinal patterns before concluding.

4. **Vocabulary mismatch.** Collapse scanners calibrated for Enron/PDVSA can't
   see simulative exploitation. If the scanner returns 0.0 on a stressed
   institution, check whether the vocabulary matches the threat type.

5. **Treating L4 as L2.** Lived institutional experience is L4 evidence — real
   but local. It cannot establish system-wide state without broader
   corroboration. "You are one sensor in one part of the system."

6. **Clean timelines on messy dynamics.** Institutions don't erode on cliffs —
   they erode on curves. Assigning "breaking point" dates to gradual processes
   is dramatic but false.

7. **Confusing corporation with institution.** A corporation can continue
   producing, earning and paying dividends while the institution — memory,
   truth transmission, technical authority, ability to discover — erodes. Read
   both.

8. **Sanitising institutional violence.** Framing dossier-building, MSS
   traps, and rightsizing as "formal mechanisms" or "restructuring" obscures
   what is actually happening to people. Name it plainly, then contextualise.
