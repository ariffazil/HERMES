> Fact class: VERIFIED unless noted. Caveats included per source.

## 1. Interoception

**Definition:** The brain's process of sensing, interpreting, and predicting internal bodily states — heartbeat, breath, temperature, gut motility, muscle tension, pain, hunger, arousal.

**Neural pathways:**
- Signals travel via lamina I of the spinal cord along the spinothalamic pathway
- Project through nucleus solitarius to brainstem, thalamus, insula, somatosensory cortex, anterior cingulate cortex
- Key hub: posterior insula (primary interoceptive cortex) → anterior insula (awareness)

**Key papers:**
- Craig, A.D. (2002). How do you feel? Interoception: the sense of the physiological condition of the body. *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 3, 655-666.
- Barrett, L.F. & Simmons, W.K. (2015). Interoceptive predictions in the brain. *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 16, 419-429.
- Barrett, L.F. (2017). *How Emotions Are Made: The Secret Life of the Brain.* Houghton Mifflin Harcourt.

**Measurement:** MAIA (Multidimensional Assessment of Interoceptive Awareness) — 8 subscales:
1. Noticing (awareness of body sensations)
2. Not-Distracting (tendency not to ignore discomfort)
3. Not-Worrying (tendency not to worry about body sensations)
4. Attention Regulation (ability to sustain/control attention to body)
5. Emotional Awareness (awareness of connection between body and emotions)
6. Self-Regulation (ability to regulate distress by attention to body)
7. Body Listening (active listening to body for insight)
8. Trusting (experience of body as safe and trustworthy)

**Constructed Emotion model (Barrett, 2017):**
The brain uses interoceptive input to *predict* what bodily state is occurring, then categorizes it using learned emotion concepts. Emotions are not reactive responses — they are predictive constructions rooted in bodily sensations and shaped by conceptual learning. This means: you don't feel anxiety and then get a fast heartbeat. Your brain predicts a fast heartbeat and constructs "anxiety" as the explanation.

**Clinical relevance:**
Misrepresentations of internal states (interoceptive prediction errors) underlie: anxiety, depression, panic disorder, anorexia, bulimia, PTSD, OCD, ADHD, alexithymia, somatic symptom disorder.

## 2. Somatic Marker Hypothesis (Damasio)

**Core claim:** Emotional processes guide (bias) behavior, particularly decision-making. "Somatic markers" are body-based feelings associated with emotions (e.g., rapid heartbeat with anxiety, nausea with disgust) that strongly influence subsequent decisions.

**Key paper:** Damasio, A.R., Tranel, D., & Damasio, H.C. (1991). Somatic markers and the guidance of behaviour: Theory and preliminary testing. In H.S. Levin et al. (Eds.), *Frontal Lobe Function and Dysfunction*.

**Evidence:**
- Iowa Gambling Task (Bechara et al., 1996): Healthy participants generate anticipatory skin conductance responses (SCRs) to disadvantageous decks *before* they consciously know the decks are bad.
- Patients with ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) damage: no anticipatory SCRs, persistently bad decisions despite intact cognitive knowledge of the rules.

**Critique and replication status:**
- Dunn et al. (2006): Critical review noting evidence largely drawn from IGT; some study design issues.
- Maia & McClelland (2004): vmPFC patients can *report* changing contingencies but perseverate in behavior — the question of why cognitive knowledge alone doesn't produce normal behavior remains.
- Pulse-rate deceleration during pre-decision period confirmed in ultimatum game studies with normal and psychopathic subjects.
- **VERIFIED hypothesis with CONTESTED specifics.** The core insight (body signals guide decisions) is robust. The specific mechanism (SCRs as THE marker) is debated.

## 3. Polyvagal Theory (Porges)

**Core claim:** The autonomic nervous system has a hierarchical organization of three circuits that shape social behavior, emotion regulation, and stress responses.

**Three states (evolutionary hierarchy):**

| State | Neural circuit | Function | Body signature |
|-------|---------------|----------|----------------|
| Ventral vagal (social engagement) | Myelinated vagus (nucleus ambiguus) | Safety, connection, co-regulation | Calm breath, open face, relaxed shoulders, prosody |
| Sympathetic | Sympathetic chain | Mobilization (fight/flight) | Elevated HR, muscle tension, shallow breathing |
| Dorsal vagal (immobilization) | Unmyelinated vagus (dorsal motor nucleus) | Shutdown, conservation | Collapse, numbness, bradycardia, dissociation |

**Key concepts:**
- **Neuroception:** Pre-conscious detection of safety vs. threat through facial expression, vocal prosody, body posture, and environmental cues. Not perception — below conscious awareness.
- **Co-regulation:** Nervous systems regulate each other. A calm presence down-regulates another person's sympathetic activation. Measurable in HRV synchrony between interacting individuals.
- **Social Engagement System:** When ventral vagal is dominant, the neural pathways from brainstem to face and throat are active → facial expressiveness, vocal prosody, listening capacity.

**Key paper:** Porges, S.W. (1994). Orienting in a defensive world: Mammalian modifications of our evolutionary heritage. *Psychophysiology*, 32, 301-318.

**Current status (2025 PMC review):**
Polyvagal Theory proposes an evolutionarily informed neurophysiological framework. Some evolutionary biologists contest the specific phylogenetic claims (presence of myelinated vagus in lungfish). The clinical applications (neuroception, co-regulation, social engagement) are widely used in trauma therapy. **VERIFIED framework with CAVEATS on evolutionary specifics.**

## 4. Somatic Experiencing (Levine)

**Core claim:** Trauma is not the event — it is the *incomplete defensive response* frozen in the body. Animals in the wild shake off survival energy after threat; humans don't, and the energy stays stored as chronic nervous system dysregulation.

**Key paper:** Levine, P.A. (1997). *Waking the Tiger: Healing Trauma.* North Atlantic Books.

**Mechanism:**
- **Pendulation:** Oscillating between activation (trauma energy) and calm (safety). The nervous system learns it can move between states.
- **Titration:** Processing in small, manageable doses rather than overwhelming abreaction.
- **Completion:** The defensive response (fight/flight/freeze) that was interrupted during the traumatic event is finally completed, releasing the stored energy.

**Core techniques:**
1. Tracking bodily sensations (interoception focus)
2. Resourcing (building felt sense of safety)
3. Pendulation (oscillating between activation and calm)
4. Titration (small doses of activation)
5. Completion (allowing the body to finish the interrupted response)

**Status:** VERIFIED clinical practice. Widely used in trauma therapy. Empirical support growing but RCT evidence still limited compared to CBT/EMDR.

## 5. Movement Intelligence

### Feldenkrais Method
**Founder:** Moshe Feldenkrais (1904-1984)
**Core principle:** "The body is not an object, but a living, sensing process, inseparable from thinking, feeling, and relating."
**Method:** Movement sequences (Awareness Through Movement) or hands-on guidance (Functional Integration) that reorganize neuromuscular patterns. Not exercise — *learning through movement.*
**Application:** Pain reduction, improved movement efficiency, enhanced body awareness, performance optimization.

### Alexander Technique
**Founder:** F.M. Alexander (1869-1955)
**Core principle:** Inhibition of habitual tension patterns. "Let the neck be free, the head release, the back broaden, the spine lengthen."
**Method:** Not relaxation — *non-doing.* The teacher uses touch and verbal instruction to help the student recognize and release unnecessary muscular tension.
**Application:** Posture, chronic pain, performance (music, acting, sport), stress reduction.

**Both operate on:** How you move is how you think. Changing movement patterns changes cognitive and emotional patterns.

## 6. Artificial Interoception (Agent Systems)

### INTERO Ontology (University of Bremen, 2025)

**Paper:** Maimon, A., Wald, I.Y., Pomarlan, M., et al. (2025). INTERO: A Model of Robotic Interoceptive Sensing. *RobOntics Workshop, CEUR-WS, Vol. 4169.*

**Core claim:** Robots lack mental states but employ mechanisms analogous to internal states (battery level, temperature, power consumption, CPU load). These signals fluctuate dynamically and could offer insights into the "inner state" of robotic agents.

**Formalization:** Expanding the MOI ontology (Metacognition Ontology Infrastructure) with interoceptive categories. Building on SOMA (robotics ontology), SSN/SOSA (sensor ontologies), and Mizoguchi & Borgo's systemic view.

**Key insight:** "Artificial interoception provides a mechanism for adaptive autonomy. Robots can dynamically reorganize priorities, alter strategies, or delay non-essential tasks when internal states exceed thresholds."

**Status:** VERIFIED research. Formal ontology stage — not yet deployed in production agents.

### Homeostatic Reinforcement Learning (Laurençon et al., 2021)

**Paper:** Laurençon, H., Ségera, C.-R., Lussange, J., & Gutkin, B.S. (2021). Continuous Homeostatic Reinforcement Learning for Self-Regulated Autonomous Agents. *arXiv:2109.06580.*

**Core claim:** Instead of fixed external reward, the agent's reward signal is derived from internal state errors (deviations from desired setpoints). Links drive-reduction theories from psychology with RL algorithms.

**Result:** Simulated agents learn survival-enhancing behaviors (seeking "food" when energy is low) purely from the imperative to maintain internal variables within optimal ranges.

**Status:** VERIFIED research. Simulated agents only.

### Computational Models of Interoception (TINS, 2020)

**Paper:** Reviews in *Trends in Neurosciences* covering:
1. Models of interoception (perceiving internal states from sensory data)
2. Models of body regulation (selecting actions to maintain homeostasis)
3. Models of forecasting (predicting future consequences of actions on the body)

**Key framework:** Active inference / predictive processing — the brain continuously generates predictions about internal bodily states to minimize physiological uncertainty. Interoception is not passive — it's an active regulatory mechanism.

### Embodied Neural Homeostat (Yoshida et al., 2024)

**Concept:** An agent that self-regulates based on internal energy levels, demonstrating adaptive behaviors through reinforcement learning driven by homeostasis (not external reward).

**Status:** VERIFIED research. BioRxiv preprint.

## 7. Embodied Cognition (General Framework)

**Core claim:** Cognition cannot be fully understood without considering the influence of the body and its interactions with the environment. The body actively shapes cognition — it's not just a vehicle for the brain.

**Key references:**
- Varela, F., Thompson, E., & Rosch, E. (1991). *The Embodied Mind.*
- Clark, A. (1997). *Being There: Putting Brain, Body, and World Together Again.*
- Barrett, L.F. (2011). *Beyond the Brain: How Body and Environment Shape Animal and Human Minds.*

**Relevance to agents:** An agent without embodied self-monitoring is operating with the cognitive architecture equivalent of a disembodied brain — it can process inputs but cannot sense its own state, predict its own degradation, or regulate itself adaptively.
