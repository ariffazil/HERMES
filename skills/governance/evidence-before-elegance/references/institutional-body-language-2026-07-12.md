# Institutional Body Language Framework

> An institution's true body language is not what it announces — it is what it repeatedly funds, permits, protects, and refuses to repair.

Origin: PETRONAS institutional audit, 2026-07-12. Arif Fazil sovereign framework.

---

## The Governing Principle

The more expensive and difficult to reverse a behaviour is, the more honestly it reveals institutional intent.

## Signal Hierarchy (read in this order)

1. **Irreversible commitments** — contracts, capital projects, constitutional changes
2. **Capital allocation** — where money actually goes
3. **Personnel and authority changes** — who gains power, who loses it
4. **Contracts and incentives** — what's rewarded, what's punished
5. **Operational behaviour** — what actually happens day-to-day
6. **Official language** — what the institution says (cheapest signal, least reliable)

## Eight Channels of Institutional Body Language

### A. Capital Posture — where the body puts its weight

Inspect: capex, maintenance spend, R&D, dividends/buybacks, acquisitions/divestments, debt repayment, hiring budgets, training, safety expenditure, political commitments.

**Key:** Don't read budget size alone. Read what is PROTECTED when pressure arrives.

### B. Institutional Breath — cadence and liquidity

Breathing appears in: payment cycles, project approval speed, procurement cadence, reporting frequency, decision backlog, hiring freezes, stretched supplier terms, emergency cash controls.

**Strained breath signs:** approvals centralise, routine payments need senior intervention, suppliers wait longer, reporting becomes more frequent but less informative, long-term work sacrificed to urgent requests.

### C. Personnel Movement — where strength is built or lost

Watch: who is promoted, who leaves voluntarily, which functions gain headcount, which are hollowed out, whether technical experts retain authority, whether loyalists replace dissenters, whether critical work becomes contractor-dependent.

**Strong signal:** Pattern of respected technical people leaving while communications/central control functions expand = institution shifting from reality production toward narrative management.

### D. Information Posture — the nervous system

Inspect: does bad news move upward quickly, do field observations survive executive filtering, do metrics change when performance worsens, does inconvenient data disappear, do leaders ask questions or only issue conclusions.

**Warning signs:** definitions change after targets missed, presentations contain more confidence but fewer raw numbers, risk registers repeat without closure, incident reports become shorter as incidents increase.

### E. Governance Posture — skeleton and joints

Read: who may break rules, whose mistakes are forgiven, whether procedures apply upward, whether committees decide or legitimise prior decisions, whether boards challenge executives, whether accountability follows authority.

**Key signal — exception geometry:** Who receives flexibility, and who receives enforcement? If senior actors bypass controls while junior actors are punished for minor deviations, the real constitution is hierarchy, not policy.

### F. Maintenance Behaviour — institutional metabolism

Look at: asset maintenance, software debt, documentation, succession planning, staff development, data quality, cybersecurity hygiene, vendor concentration, emergency reserves, operational redundancy.

**Warning:** Institutions under hidden stress launch new initiatives while old systems decay, announce transformation while core records remain unreliable, expand assets without maintaining existing ones.

### G. Boundary Behaviour — how the institution treats others

Observe: payment behaviour toward small suppliers, contract asymmetry, employee grievance handling, treatment of whistleblowers, negotiation behaviour, liability transfer, environmental externalities.

**Key questions:** Who receives the benefit? Who carries the downside? Who has the right to stop the action? Who has no voice but absorbs the consequences?

### H. Crisis Reflex — what happens before the institution can perform

During pressure watch: who is protected first, which budget is cut first, whether leaders centralise or distribute authority, whether facts are released or controlled, whether blame moves downward, whether losses are socialised while gains remain concentrated.

## Regulatory States

| State | Description |
|---|---|
| **NOMINAL** | Information flows, maintenance continues, decisions match declared purpose |
| **GUARDED** | More approvals, tighter information control, reduced tolerance for dissent |
| **OVERLOADED** | Backlogs rise, urgent work consumes strategic work, maintenance deferred |
| **NUMB** | Incidents and complaints no longer alter behaviour. Centre stops responding to pain |
| **CAPTURED** | Subgroup receives disproportionate benefit and controls information/rules/appointments |
| **FRAGMENTED** | Units optimise locally while institution loses common direction |
| **DECAYING** | Institution consumes inherited assets, trust, capability faster than it replaces them |
| **REGENERATING** | Admits damage, restores information flow, repairs capability, changes incentives |

## Simulative vs Extractive Patterns

**Extractive:** Internal theft, fraud, asset stripping. Tools calibrated for Enron/PDVSA/1MDB patterns. Detection is reasonably good.

**Simulative:** Institution extracts from stakeholders through legal architecture, constitutional position, or structural advantage while technically operating "legally." NOCs outside standard corporate governance, sovereign instruments with constitutional monopoly rights, institutions that substitute themselves for fiscal capacity.

**The diagnostic gap:** Tools calibrated for extractive patterns return INSUFFICIENT_SIGNAL or LOW when scanning simulative institutions. The vocabulary doesn't recognise "operates outside Companies Act" or "constitutional monopoly" as capture indicators.

**Rule:** When tools return all-clear but institutional signals suggest otherwise, check the extraction pattern. If simulative, the tool's vocabulary is the blindspot.

## The Four-Reading Discipline

Every institutional signal should be read through four layers:

1. **Observation** — What happened?
2. **Possible meaning** — What might explain it?
3. **Corroboration** — What else changed?
4. **Verification** — What evidence can distinguish the explanations?

Institutional somatic intelligence is not intuition alone. It is intuition disciplined by verification.

## The Ten Revealing Questions

1. What does it protect when resources become scarce?
2. What truth can no one safely say?
3. Who is allowed to fail without consequence?
4. Who carries responsibility without authority?
5. Which assets are being consumed but not renewed?
6. What problem keeps returning under different names?
7. What decision is repeatedly delayed because acting would expose the real conflict?
8. Where do official values and actual incentives diverge?
9. Who benefits from institutional confusion?
10. What would the institution do if nobody were watching?

## Alignment Diagnostic

| Surface | Question |
|---|---|
| **Narrative** | What does the institution say? |
| **Capital** | What does it fund? |
| **Authority** | Who receives power? |
| **Consequence** | Who carries the cost? |

**Healthy alignment:** "We prioritise operational integrity" → maintenance funding rises, technical leaders gain authority, incidents trigger repair, bonuses include integrity metrics.

**Institutional dissonance:** "People are our greatest asset" → training cut, turnover rises, workloads increase, executive rewards protected, employee feedback disappears.

---

## Baseline Matters More Than Gesture

The strongest signals are deviations from the institution's OWN baseline. Ask:
- Is this new?
- Is it accelerating?
- Does it appear across multiple units?
- Is it temporary or becoming structural?
- Does official language acknowledge it?
- Does capital behaviour confirm it?

Longitudinal reading is stronger than snapshot reading.
