# NSPW Mud Canopy — Literature Reference Bank

## Key Papers

### Morley et al. (2023) — The Mud Canopy Paper
- **Citation:** Morley, C.K., Promrak, W., et al. (2023). "A major Miocene deepwater mud canopy system: The North Sabah–Pagasa Wedge, northwestern Borneo." *Geosphere*, 19(1), 291–334. DOI: 10.1130/GES02518.1
- **Key findings:**
  - NSPW reinterpreted as mobile shale system, NOT tectonic thrust sheet
  - ~10.5 Ma: short burst of submarine mud volcanism, ~50 centres, 4-80 km² each, total canopy ~1,900 km²
  - Canopy affected deposition of Rotan Sands turbidite reservoirs
  - Second mud-canopy system described globally; largest and most complex
  - Analogous to Sigsbee salt canopy system
  - One well drilled NSPW upper part: ~350m alternating shales and sandstones

### Gilligan et al. (2026) — nBOSS Receiver Functions
- **Citation:** Gilligan, A., Cornwell, D., et al. (2026). "Evidence of Subduction, Collision, and Extension in Northern Borneo: Constraints From Receiver Functions." *JGR: Solid Earth*. DOI: 10.1029/2025JB031499
- **Key findings:**
  - 46 broadband seismometers across Sabah (2018-2020)
  - Crustal thickness: 24-60 km across Sabah
  - 60 km beneath Crocker Range = underthrust Dangerous Grounds continental crust
  - 24 km in central Sabah = Miocene extension
  - Dipping low-velocity anomalies = DG underthrust evidence

### Pilia et al. (2023) — Lithospheric Drip
- **Citation:** Pilia, S., Davies, D.R., Hall, R., et al. (2023). "Post-subduction tectonics induced by extension from a lithospheric drip." *Nature Geoscience*, 16, 646–652. DOI: 10.1038/s41561-023-01201-7
- **Key findings:**
  - Two distinct mantle anomalies: PSCS slab remnant + lithospheric drip from Semporna Peninsula
  - Drip-driven extension explains 24 km crustal thinning in central Sabah
  - Post-subduction tectonics driven by extension, not compression
  - **HYPOTHESIS:** drip may affect NSPW overpressure timing — not uniquely constrained

### Hall (2017) — Proto-South China Sea
- **Citation:** Hall, R. (2017). "Nature and demise of the Proto-South China Sea." *Bulletin of the Geological Society of Malaysia*.
- **Key findings:**
  - PSCS restricted to slab subducted beneath Sabah/Cagayan, Eocene-Early Miocene
  - Western limit: West Baram Line
  - PSCS slab imaged in lower mantle via P-wave tomography
  - No equivalent oceanic slab subduction beneath Sarawak

### Greenfield et al. (2022) — Surface Wave Tomography
- **Citation:** Greenfield, T., et al. (2022). "Post-Subduction Tectonics of Sabah, Northern Borneo, Inferred From Surface Wave Tomography." *Geophysical Research Letters*. DOI: 10.1029/2021GL096117

## Tectonic Sequence (Consensus)

```
~44 Ma: PSCS oceanic subduction begins
~21 Ma: PSCS subduction terminates (Hall 2017, Gilligan 2026)
~21-15 Ma: Dangerous Grounds continental underthrust
~15-11 Ma: Collision + NSPW wedge loading by deltaics
~10.5 Ma: MUD CANOPY EVENT — overpressure release
~9 Ma: Celebes Sea subduction terminates
~9-10 Ma: Lithospheric drip (Pilia 2023) → central Sabah extension
~9-8 Ma: Rotan Sands turbidite reservoir deposition
~8 Ma-present: Flexural/gravity-driven deformation (no active subduction)
```

## Crust Type Consensus

**Continental margin with fossil oceanic slab at depth.**
- NOT active oceanic-crust subduction
- NOT simple oceanic basin
- Fossil PSCS slab imaged as high-velocity mantle body (tomography)
- Dangerous Grounds continental crust underthrust (receiver functions, up to 60 km)
- NW Borneo Trough = flexural/gravity-driven, NOT active trench
