# NW Sabah Deepwater Geological Framework

Condensed from: GEOX session 2026-07-07, Morley 2023, Hall PSCS, nBOSS, Khamis 2017/2018, Madon 2015, Abdullah 2017, TGS MC3D.

## Tectonic Framework

**Crust type:** Continental margin with fossil PSCS oceanic slab at depth (CLAIM).
- Crust up to ~60 km beneath Crocker Range, thinning to ~24 km in central Sabah (nBOSS receiver functions)
- Dangerous Grounds continental block underthrust beneath NW Borneo after PSCS subduction
- NW Borneo Trough = flexural/gravity feature, NOT active trench
- Setap Shale = regional detachment for gravity-driven shortening

**PSCS subduction:** Oceanic plate subducted beneath NW Borneo, Eocene–Early Miocene (~44–21 Ma). West Baram Line = western limit. Slab fully consumed by ~21 Ma. High-velocity remnant imaged in mantle tomography. Celebes Sea subduction terminated ~9 Ma (Gilligan 2026).

**Lithospheric drip (HYPOTHESIS):** Pilia et al. (2023, Nature Geoscience) propose a lithospheric drip from beneath the Semporna Peninsula drove Miocene extension and crustal thinning in central Sabah (24 km crust). This drip model explains: (1) the compression→extension transition at ~10.5 Ma, (2) accommodation space for turbidite reservoir deposition, and (3) complex geothermal gradients. ## NSPW (North Sabah–Pagasa Wedge)

**What it is:** Post-subduction shale-rich wedge of Eocene–Middle Miocene sediments. World's largest mud canopy system (Morley et al. 2023, Geosphere).

**Key facts:**
- ~50 mud volcano centres, each 4–80 km²
- Total mud canopy area: ~1,900 km²
- Built during short burst at ~10.5 Ma (Middle Miocene)
- Mud canopies directly affected deposition of Rotan-type turbidite reservoirs
- Second mud canopy system described globally; largest and most complex
- Analogous to salt canopy systems (Sigsbee) — gravity-driven mobile material

**Mechanism:** Overpressure from deltaic loading + source rock maturation → shale mobility → mud volcanism → canopy extrusion

**Mud volcanism ≠ igneous:** Post-subduction, heat and fluids are focused in sedimentary wedges and faults, not arc magma systems. Overpressured mud + gas finds easier paths to surface than new melt, producing mud volcano fields instead of stratovolcano chains. Causal chain: old subduction → thick gas-rich sediment wedge + fossil slab → collision/compression + faults → overpressure in shales → mud volcanoes.

## Structural Trends (DWFTB)

Three major toe-thrust trends:
1. **M-La-S** (Maling–Laurel–Sotong): Fore-thrust dominant, Rotan/Buluh
2. **L-B-P** (Limbayong–Bestari–Block P): Thin-skinned toe-thrust, Bestari/Block P prospects
3. **Pg-Lt-U** (Pagasa–Limbang–Ugis): Backthrust interference, northern gas

**Structural evolution:** Two phases:
- **Constructive:** Forward-breaking thrusts, trap formation, oil charge
- **Destructive:** Backthrusts, seal breach, re-migration, late gas charge (explains northern gas dominance)

## Reservoir Architecture

**Pre-kinematic vs Syn-kinematic:**
- Pre-kinematic (Kebabangan, Kinarut): unconfined turbidites, wide lateral extent
- Syn-kinematic (Kamunsu, younger): structurally ponded, thick in mini-basins, thin on crest

**Key insight:** Reservoir distribution controlled by timing of deposition vs structural growth. Pre-kinematic = stratigraphic play. Syn-kinematic = structural + combination.

**Additional complexity:** West Crocker Formation (onshore analog) shows linked debrites and injectites — vertical fluid conduits impacting reservoir connectivity.

## Petroleum System

- **Source:** Middle Miocene, Type II/III kerogen from MTC-transported organic matter (Abdullah 2017)
- **Reservoir:** Kebabangan, Kinarut, Kamunsu sandstones (Miocene turbidites)
- **Seal:** Marine shales + NSPW mud canopy sheets
- **Trap:** Fold-thrust anticlines (fault-propagation folds)
- **Charge timing:** Oil expelled during early thrusting, gas post-peak thrusting. Re-migration during structural evolution.
- **Critical uncertainty:** Mature source rock kitchens not well-defined; charge mechanism remains key risk
